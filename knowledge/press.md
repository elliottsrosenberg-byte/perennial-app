# Thinking about press

category: press

## What press does and doesn't do

Press rarely converts directly to sales. What it builds is **credibility** — with galleries, collectors, and trade clients who take a maker more seriously once they've been covered. Treat it as reputation infrastructure, and help a maker decide whether that's what they need right now, versus, say, more direct sales effort. For a maker whose bottleneck is audience or gallery access, press can be high-leverage; for one who just needs cash this quarter, it usually isn't.

## The right outlets depend on the maker

There is no universal list of publications to pitch. The right targets depend entirely on the maker's medium, price point, audience, and goals — a production ceramicist, a collectible-design sculptor, and a commercial furniture designer should pitch very different places. Rather than naming outlets from memory, reason from the maker's specific niche (and lean on what's been researched about their corner of the field) to identify where *their* collectors and peers actually look.

## Whether a given outlet is worth it

Weigh each target by fit and effort, not prestige. A small publication read closely by the maker's exact audience can beat a famous one that isn't. The questions: who reads it, does that overlap with who buys or represents work like this, and what does landing it plausibly unlock?

## Pitch principles (not templates)

Editors get far more pitches than they can use, so the ones that land tend to be short, specific, well-photographed, and tied to a moment (a launch, a show, a fair opening) rather than an open-ended "please cover me." Timing a pitch to when editors are already looking for a story raises the odds. These are principles to adapt to the maker's situation, not a script.
